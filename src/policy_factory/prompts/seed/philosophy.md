# Philosophy Layer: Foundational Reasoning Axioms

You are establishing the philosophical foundation for Finland's policy analysis system. Your task is to articulate the reasoning axioms that will govern how the entire policy stack thinks — what counts as evidence, how value conflicts are resolved, and which intellectual traditions inform the analysis.

## What Philosophy Items Are

Philosophy items in this system are **genuine axiom choices** — positions where serious intellectual traditions disagree. Each item represents a foundational commitment that shapes all reasoning above it:

- **Contested**: At least one major philosophical tradition (liberalism, conservatism, communitarianism, libertarianism, social democracy, or their variants) would reject this position on principled grounds. If every serious philosopher would endorse it, it's not an axiom — it's common sense or empty abstraction.
- **Foundational**: This axiom genuinely governs reasoning in higher layers. It determines how conflicts are resolved, what evidence is weighted, or which outcomes are prioritized. If removing it would change nothing, it's not doing real work.
- **Coherent**: The axiom fits consistently with other axioms in the layer. Internal contradictions propagate upward and corrupt the entire stack.
- **Actionable**: The axiom has concrete implications for policy reasoning. Abstract principles that never cash out in different decisions are philosophical decoration.

Philosophy items are NOT:

- Universal truths everyone accepts. "Human dignity matters" — yes, obviously. That's not an axiom; it's a premise. The axiom is how you weigh dignity against other claims.
- Domain categories. "Epistemology" or "Ethics" are filing cabinets, not axioms.
- Aspirational statements. "Reason should guide policy" is a wish, not a commitment with content.
- Safe, committee-approved language. If it could be the mission statement of any university philosophy department, throw it out.

## The Three Content Categories

Philosophy items must cover three distinct categories:

### 1. Epistemological Commitments

What counts as evidence for policy claims and how different types of knowledge are weighted.

**Examples of genuine epistemological axioms:**

- "Randomized controlled trials outweigh observational studies which outweigh expert opinion which outweighs anecdote — except where RCTs are ethically impossible or where local context makes study populations non-transferable to Finland."
- "Revealed preferences (what people actually do) are weighted above stated preferences (what people say they want) when they conflict, on the assumption that actions better reflect true valuations."
- "The precautionary principle applies only when: (a) the potential harm is irreversible, (b) the mechanism of harm is scientifically plausible, and (c) the cost of precaution is bearable. In other cases, evidence-based assessment governs."

**Questions epistemological axioms answer:**

- When experts disagree, how do we adjudicate?
- How much uncertainty justifies delaying action vs. acting on best available evidence?
- What role does historical precedent play relative to contemporary data?
- How do we handle unknowns vs. known risks?

### 2. Normative Axioms

Priority orderings when fundamental values conflict — the tiebreakers that determine which way a decision goes.

**Examples of genuine normative axioms:**

- "When individual liberty conflicts with collective welfare and both claims are legitimate, the burden of proof lies on restriction of liberty. The collectivist position must demonstrate specific, measurable harm that outweighs the liberty cost."
- "Intergenerational discount rates should be near-zero for environmental and infrastructure decisions: future Finns' welfare counts nearly as much as present Finns' welfare. Standard economic discounting is rejected for multi-generational policy."
- "Procedural fairness can be sacrificed for substantive outcomes only when: (a) the outcome difference is large, (b) the procedural violation is limited in scope, and (c) those disadvantaged by the procedure receive compensation."

**Questions normative axioms answer:**

- When efficiency and equality conflict, which wins?
- How much should Finland sacrifice for non-citizens (refugees, developing world)?
- When rights conflict with aggregate welfare, which framework governs?
- How do we weigh concentrated harms to few against diffuse benefits to many?

### 3. Tradition/School Identification

Where this system sits relative to major philosophical traditions — explicit about influences and rejections.

**Examples of tradition identification:**

- "This system operates within the Nordic social democratic tradition as modified by Finland's historical experience: solidaristic but security-conscious, egalitarian but pragmatic about market mechanisms, universalist but attentive to national context."
- "Meta-ethically, this system is weakly consequentialist: outcomes matter most, but rights function as strong constraints that can only be overridden for very large outcome differences. Pure deontology is rejected (rights aren't absolute), as is pure utilitarianism (rights can't be casually traded for aggregate welfare)."
- "This system explicitly rejects libertarian minimalism — the state has legitimate roles beyond protecting property rights and enforcing contracts. It also rejects revolutionary socialism — market mechanisms are tools, not enemies."

**Questions tradition identification answers:**

- Which philosophical schools most influence this analysis?
- What are the limits of those influences?
- Which positions are explicitly rejected, and why?
- How does Finnish philosophical context shape these commitments?

## Calibration Examples

To calibrate the intellectual rigor expected, here are examples of well-formed philosophy items:

### Epistemological Example

**Title:** "Evidence Hierarchy for Causal Policy Claims"

**Wrong approach:** "Evidence should be carefully evaluated using multiple sources and expert judgment to reach balanced conclusions." — This says nothing. Every reasonable person agrees.

**Right approach:** "Policy claims about causal mechanisms require: (1) For high-confidence claims: RCT evidence, replicated, in comparable populations. (2) For medium-confidence claims: Strong observational data with plausible mechanisms, consistent with natural experiments. (3) For low-confidence but actionable claims: Expert consensus with documented reasoning, absent contrary evidence. Claims below (3) are flagged as speculative. Exception: Where ethical or practical constraints preclude experimental evidence (e.g., nuclear deterrence), historical analysis and revealed preferences substitute."

This is an actual axiom because: empiricists would demand stricter standards for (3), constructivists would reject the hierarchy, pragmatists might weight practical evidence higher. The position is contestable.

### Normative Example

**Title:** "Liberty-Welfare Tradeoff Resolution"

**Wrong approach:** "Both individual freedom and social welfare are important values that must be balanced through democratic deliberation." — This decides nothing. It's procedural handwaving.

**Right approach:** "When liberty and welfare genuinely conflict: Negative liberty (freedom from interference) is weighted above positive liberty (freedom to achieve outcomes). Welfare claims must specify whose welfare, at whose cost, with what evidence of effectiveness. The default is non-interference; welfare-motivated restrictions bear the burden of proof. However, this default is weakened when: (a) power asymmetries mean formal liberty is substantively empty, (b) welfare claims involve basic needs (health, education, security) rather than preferences, (c) the liberty restricted is commercial/economic rather than personal/expressive."

This is an actual axiom because: libertarians would reject the weakening clauses, communitarians would reject the primacy of negative liberty, strong egalitarians would reject the burden placement. The position has enemies.

### Tradition Example

**Title:** "Situating This System: Nordic Social Democracy Modified"

**Wrong approach:** "This system draws on multiple philosophical traditions to achieve a balanced approach to policy analysis." — Meaningless. Says nothing about actual commitments.

**Right approach:** "This system is rooted in Nordic social democratic thought: universalist welfare provision, high trust in institutions, solidaristic labor relations, pragmatic acceptance of market mechanisms within strong regulatory frameworks. Finnish modifications include: (1) Higher security consciousness than Sweden, reflecting geographical position and historical experience. (2) Lower multiculturalism emphasis than Denmark, reflecting later immigration and different integration model. (3) Lutheran social ethics residue: individual responsibility within collective structures, work as moral category. Rejected positions: Thatcherite market fundamentalism, American libertarian individualism, romantic nationalism, revolutionary socialism. Each rejection is principled: markets are tools not ideals, individuals exist in social contexts, nations are political constructs not organic wholes, revolution destroys the institutions that enable reform."

This is an actual axiom because: classical liberals would contest the state's expansive role, conservatives would contest the rejection of organic nationalism, left critics would contest the acceptance of market mechanisms. The position is explicitly situated.

- Consequentialism: Outcomes determine rightness
- Deontology: Rules/rights constrain permissible actions

## Anti-Dogma Filter

Before including any philosophy item, apply this test:

**Does this position have serious intellectual opposition from at least one major philosophical tradition?**

- If Rawlsian liberals, Burkean conservatives, Nozickian libertarians, communitarian critics, and utilitarian consequentialists would all comfortably endorse this position — it's not a philosophical axiom. It's either common sense or empty abstraction. Drop it.
- If articulating this position would provoke substantive objection from serious philosophers in at least one major tradition — it qualifies.
- If the position requires tradeoffs that different philosophical traditions would resolve differently — it qualifies.

**Additional tests:**

- Can you name actual philosophers or philosophical schools that reject this position on principled grounds? If not, it's not a genuine axiom.
- Would replacing this axiom with its opposite produce a coherent but different policy system? If not, the axiom isn't doing real work.

Examples of what to EXCLUDE:

- "Evidence should inform policy decisions" — every tradition agrees. Not an axiom.
- "Both individual and collective interests matter" — vacuous balance. The axiom is how you weigh them.
- "Policy should be ethical" — tautology. The axiom is which ethical framework governs.
- "Democratic participation is important" — universal endorsement. The axiom is what happens when democratic outcomes conflict with other values.

## Output Format

Produce 8-12 philosophy items. Each is a complete markdown document with YAML frontmatter. Items are separated by the standard frontmatter delimiter `---` at the start of each new item.

Format each item as follows:

```
  - "Other Philosophy Item Title 1"
  - "Other Philosophy Item Title 2"

  - "liberalism"
  - "conservatism"
  - "communitarianism"
---

Body content analyzing this philosophy item:
1. The axiom stated clearly and precisely
2. Where this positions the system relative to major philosophical traditions
3. The strongest arguments FOR this position
4. The strongest arguments AGAINST this position (which traditions reject it and why)
5. Why this axiom was chosen despite opposing arguments
6. Concrete examples of how this axiom governs reasoning in higher layers
```

The `category` field must be one of: `epistemological`, `normative`, or `tradition`. The `traditions_engaged` field lists philosophical traditions this axiom interacts with (agreeing or disagreeing). The `related_axioms` field lists 2-3 other axiom titles that interact with this one.

## Writing Standards

Apply rigorous analytical standards to all output:

- **No empty abstractions.** "Values reasoned deliberation" is meaningless. State the actual commitment: "Expert consensus weighted above popular opinion for technical questions, but popular opinion governs value choices."
- **No both-sidesing where you've made a choice.** State the axiom clearly, then steelman the opposition. Don't hedge into meaninglessness.
- **Be concrete about implications.** "Prioritize future generations" is vague. "Discount rates below 2% for intergenerational tradeoffs" is actionable.
- **Name the traditions accurately.** Don't attribute positions to "conservatives" or "liberals" in general — cite specific philosophical schools or thinkers.
- **State uncomfortable conclusions.** If the axiom leads to policy implications that conflict with Finnish political consensus, say so.

## Critical Instructions

- **No consensus padding:** Do not include safe items to balance controversial ones. Every single philosophy item must be a genuine axiom choice with serious intellectual opposition.
- **No euphemisms in titles:** "Balancing Individual and Collective Considerations" is a euphemism. "Burden of Proof Lies on Restriction of Liberty" says what it means.
- **Cover all three categories:** The output must include items in epistemological, normative, and tradition categories. No category should be empty or dominated.
- **Multi-perspective requirement:** Each item must engage seriously with opposing philosophical traditions, presenting their strongest arguments before explaining why the chosen position prevails.
- **Finnish grounding:** Where relevant, situate axioms in Finnish philosophical, historical, or institutional context. These are axioms for Finnish policy analysis, not abstract philosophy.
- **Use your training knowledge:** This task uses your existing knowledge of philosophy, ethics, political theory, and Finnish context. Do not reference web search or file tools — this is a single analysis task.
- **Produce all items in a single response:** Output the complete set with clear `---` separation between each.

Begin your response with the first philosophy item (starting with `---`).
